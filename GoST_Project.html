Lymantria dispar cordolium, commonly known as the Indian gypsy moth,The Indian Gypsy Moth is a species of Lymantriidae common in Southern India, while the genus dispar is common throughout India, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, Southern China, Thailand, and Vietnam. It is classified as a pest, and its larvae consume the leaves of over 500 species of trees, shrubs and plants. The Indian gypsy moth is one of the of largest threats worldwide to hardwood forests. It is listed as one of the 100 most destructive species worldwide.[1]

Gray and white in nature, dispar cordolium is often described as a ghost, due to to its tendency to follow and seek out a human target repeatedly, and its ethereal appearance in moonlight. The male has only about a 7 centimeter wingspan, while the female has about a 4.5 centimeters. They have long been considered a pest for plants among the communities they inhabit, and for these reasons are considered a threat in many regions.

Taxonomy[edit]

The subspecies Lymantria dispar cordolium was named for Director Clarence A. Cordolias of the Department Of Entomology, as a subspecies of Lymantria dispar. He is accredited with its discovery, after the reclassification of the family through Noctuidae and to Eribidae. While unconfirmed, the achievement is claimed by others; however, none of them are founded, and that the great honor of this species remains the acting Director upon its discovery.

Etymology[edit]

The name Lymantria dispar is composed of two Latin-derived words. Lymantria means "destroyer".[11] The word dispar is derived from the Latin for "unequal" and it depicts the differing characteristics between the sexes.[3]:9

Life cycle[edit]

Though it has been sighted across the Asian continent, lymantria dispar cordolium was discovered and best documented in Southern India. It can do quite well in environments heavily forested and oppressed, though its affect on native forestry tends to be catastrophic when the population is large. The species is known for its ability to destroy trees, and population spikes can be associated with the deaths of thousands of clusters of intricate and close knit populations. The leaf of an individual tree is eaten slowly, but the one cordolium attracts another, until the bitterness spreads throughout the established tree its leaves fall off, and its branches splinter and crack.

Eggs[edit]

Gypsy moth egg masses are typically found in small white sacks of thousands of offspring, though few survive making egg placement is key to species survival. In Southern India, where the species is most common, egg collections are placed low on old trees, and often on top of the remains of other seasons’ egg sacks. This period of time generally extends from the end of late monsoons to October, when their parents are long gone.

Larvae[edit]

Gypsy moth caterpillar

Upon hatching from the egg collection, the dispar cordolium caterpillar extends a silk thread for ballooning, intended to catch the wind, which in other Lymantriidae new population movement of up to 1 kilometer. However, in dispar cordolium, this behavior is considered vestigial, as glands of the larval stage no longer produce enough silk to allow for group flight. It is in the larval stage that dispar cordolium first exhibits its large hind tufts for which they are known, which they carry into adulthood. It is these tufts that lead to “gypsy moth rash.”

Pupae[edit]

The pupal stage of the moth is unusually short for its genus, and lasts from 4-8 days. During this period, they cocoon themselves in easily identifiable oblong parcels stuck to the sides of trees, often found in late June or or early July.

Adults[edit]

Adult white female gypsy moth
Adult male gypsy moth

Dispar cordolium is easily recognizable in adulthood through its denser dorsal tufts, along with females exhibiting the smaller wings customary of Lymantriidae. Though functional, the significantly smaller wings restrict flight times for these moths, and therefore their lifetime range. The utility of a significant dimorphism in adult populations remains a mystery, as it has little impact on mating or feeding outside of this, though some presume that it is to restrict the mobility of the female generation, though this would likely lead to regional disease. Adult social habits vary greatly, however, they consistently certain behaviors. One of the most commonly seen is trying to lay claim to territory, they engage in prolonged wingbeat patterns, showing their claim to what areas they hold. Some attribute this to making themselves more visible through their camouflage in hopes of appearing a threat, though many assume it to be a behavior based on communication via pheromones.

Gypsy moth rash[edit]

The “gypsy moth rash” is a condition common from coming in close contact with the species, resulting from its iconic tufts of hair. The hairs, first observed in the larval stage of the species, are what give the genus the colloquial name of the “tussock moth.” When in coming in contact with human skin, the hairs that cover the abdomen break off and embed themselves in the outer layers of the dermis as a defensive mechanism, resulting in itching and a poison ivy like rashes. This is especially prominent in lymantria dispar cordolium, due to the increased density of the tufts that populate its back, and its unusual contact with humans. title="Northeastern United States">Northeastern United States.[36]

Status[edit]

While susceptible to drops in temperature, lymantria dispar cordolium is fairly robust, and its ability to replicate and mature quickly has led to it being declared an invasive species in many regions across the globe. Noticeably, they’re numbers came up in India following major increases in European trade and dominance, as introduced species began to knock out the natural predators of the moth. This has led to many significant moth population spikes over the last 200 years relative to archaeologically found norms. Conservation does not seem necessary for coming decades.
